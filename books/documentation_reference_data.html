Reference data#

The set of reference data and associated sources is given in the following. The detailed value can also be found on the graphical user interface in the tab Data.

Air transport#

The various air traffic data are derived from the ICAO (International Civil Aviation Organisation), such as annual passenger numbers, Revenue Passenger Kilometrer (RPK) or aircraft load factor.

The data on world kerosene consumption by the aviation sector are from the International Energy Agency (IEA). However, not all kerosene is consumed by commercial aviation alone. According to S. Gössling and A. Humpe in The global scale, distribution and growth of aviation: Implications for climate change, military aviation consumes 8% of kerosene and general and private aviation 4%.

Then, the coefficients for the different pollutants (also called EI for Emission Index) were taken from [LFS+21]. They make it possible to obtain different emissions (NOx, SOx…) depending on fuel consumption (or emissions of CO2).

Finally, the coefficient corresponding to other life cycle emissions (excluding combustion and fuel production), of the order of 2%, was obtained by taking an intermediate value from the studies of Melo et al. in 2020 in Sustainability Assessment and Engineering of Emerging Aircraft Technologies — Challenges, Methods and Tools.

Using these data and including indirect CO2 emissions, commercial aviation was responsible for 2.6% of global CO2 emissions in 2019. Also including non-CO2 effects, aviation (including private and military) generated 3.8% of the effective radiative forcing between 1750 and 2018 and commercial aviation is responsible for 5.1% of the increase in effective radiative forcing over a more recent period (2000-2018).

Effective radiative forcing#

Data on effective radiative forcing of aviation are obtained using the work of Lee et al. in The contribution of global aviation to anthropogenic climate forcing for 2000 to 2018. This paper quantifies the effects of aviation on the climate using the notion of effective radiative forcing. Thus, the climate impact of each contributor can be approximated by multiplying the amount of emissions (or the total distance for contrails) with a factor obtained from the data of Lee et al. The factors considered are as follows:

  • One for CO2 emissions, considering cumulative and not annual emissions to take into account the long lifetime of this gas in the atmosphere, expressed in $W/m^2/gCO_{2cum}$
  • One for NOx emissions, expressed in $W/m^2/gNOx$
  • One for water vapour emissions, expressed in $W/m^2/gH_2O$
  • Two for aerosols: one for SOx emissions, expressed in $W/m^2/gSO_2$; one for black carbon emissions, expressed in $W/m^2/gBC$
  • One for contrails, expressed in $W/m^2/km$ where the distance corresponds to the total distance travelled by aircraft annually

Others#

The coefficients to obtain the CO2 emissions associated with the production and combustion of kerosene have been extracted from the carbon base from Agence française de la transition écologique called ADEME. The annual global CO2 emissions, including fossil fuel combustion and deforestation activities, come from Global Carbon Project while annual global GHG emissions, including land-use change, are from Emissions Gap Report 2020 from ONU.
